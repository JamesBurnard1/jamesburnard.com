Exploring County-Level Access to Credit in the United States

A county-level exploratory analysis of credit score, credit card balance, and 90-day delinquency data from Opportunity Insights.

Jupyter Notebook Python Pandas Seaborn SciPy Decision Trees

Portfolio Summary

This project, created with Peter Koumpikov, explores trends in credit scores, credit balances, and debt delinquency across United States counties. We were interested in whether county-level credit outcomes could reveal geographic and demographic disparities that matter for policy, regulation, and financial inclusion.

Data Preparation

We combined three Opportunity Insights datasets: average credit score, average credit card balance, and 90-day delinquency rate. Each dataset included parent state, parent county, child race, parent income percentile, the credit variable of interest, and county name. We averaged key features across counties, joined the datasets, renamed columns for readability, and built an analysis-ready county-level table.

Exploratory Analysis

The statistical summary compared means, medians, ranges, and interquartile ranges for credit score, credit balance, and delinquency rate. Credit scores were relatively tightly distributed across counties, while credit card balances and delinquency rates showed more spread. That variation suggested that debt burden and delinquency were worth studying more closely.

We created density plots and scatter plots to compare credit score, credit balance, and delinquency. The strongest visible relationship was between credit score and delinquency rate: counties and county-race subgroups with higher credit scores tended to have lower delinquency rates.

Association Analysis

I focused on the association analysis and predictive modeling portions of the project. We created a correlation matrix, then visualized the three most important relationships: credit score and delinquency rate, credit balance and credit score, and credit balance and delinquency rate. Adding race as a dimension revealed notable differences across county-race subgroups.

In the scatter plots, observations labeled Black appeared more often in areas with higher delinquency and lower credit scores, while observations labeled Asian more often appeared in areas with lower delinquency and higher credit scores. These patterns do not prove causality, but they do motivate further study of systemic inequities in access to credit.

Predictive Modeling

For modeling, I built a decision tree classifier to classify counties as high or low delinquency using a median split. The first model included delinquency rate itself and performed strongly, but it was too easy because the target label came directly from that variable.

The stronger version removed raw delinquency rate and relied on remaining predictors like credit score, credit balance, and county identifier. Accuracy declined to 0.92, but the model became more meaningful because it inferred delinquency class from related financial characteristics rather than from the answer itself.

Key Takeaway

The project found a strong negative relationship between credit score and delinquency rate, visible both statistically and visually. It also showed that adding race as a dimension can uncover patterns that a county-only average may hide.

Future work could connect county-level credit access to quality of life, health, crime, and broader socioeconomic outcomes.
